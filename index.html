Air transport systems

Aviation Networks

Aviation networks connect airports, aircraft, crews, passengers, cargo, and information into one moving system. Their shape determines how quickly people and goods can cross regions, continents, and oceans.

What Is an Aviation Network?

An aviation network is the pattern of routes and relationships that links airports together. Each airport acts as a node, each flight route acts as an edge, and airlines design schedules so demand, aircraft availability, safety rules, and operating costs all line up.

Major hubs concentrate traffic, while regional spokes extend reach and make smaller markets accessible.

H

Hub-and-Spoke

Passengers from many smaller cities connect through a hub, helping airlines fill aircraft and offer more destination pairs.

P

Point-to-Point

Direct flights link cities without a transfer, reducing travel time and simplifying operations on dense routes.

R

Resilience

Weather, maintenance, congestion, and security events can ripple through schedules, so redundancy and recovery planning matter.

Why Networks Matter

The design of an aviation network influences ticket availability, travel time, airport growth, regional trade, emergency response, tourism, and supply-chain speed.

For Airlines

Network planning balances demand forecasts, aircraft range, crew scheduling, gate capacity, and profitable route timing.

For Cities

Strong air links can improve business access, attract visitors, support specialized cargo, and connect local economies to global markets.

Aviation networks are living systems: every route choice changes how the whole map moves.
